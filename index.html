Chapter 3: Private, Public and Global Enterprises

1. Departmental Undertaking

It is the oldest and most traditional form of public sector enterprises. These are directly managed by government departments under a ministry.

Example: Indian Railways, Post Office.

Advantages:
  • Government control ensures public accountability.
  • Revenue generated goes directly to the government.
  • Helps in national interest services like defence, railways, etc.
Disadvantages:
  • No operational flexibility.
  • Delays due to bureaucratic red-tapism.
  • Political interference is high.

2. Statutory Corporation

It is created by a special act of Parliament or State Legislature. It has a separate legal identity and is financially independent.

Example: LIC, Air India (before privatization), FCI.

Advantages:
  • Operates with independence and flexibility.
  • Free from political influence in daily operations.
  • Quick decisions and efficient operations.
Disadvantages:
  • Rigid structure due to statutory nature.
  • Often delays in appointments and decisions due to government rules.

3. Government Company

A company in which at least 51% shares are owned by the government. It is registered under the Companies Act.

Example: BHEL, ONGC, SAIL.

Advantages:
  • Easy to form compared to statutory corporations.
  • Can work like a private company with more autonomy.
  • Less red tape, more flexibility in operations.
Disadvantages:
  • Still controlled indirectly by the government.
  • Lack of full transparency and audit like pure private firms.

4. Changing Role of Public Sector

Initially, the public sector played a key role in development, but now its role has shifted towards regulation and service delivery due to privatization and liberalization.

Importance:
  • Focus on strategic areas like defence, railways.
  • Acts as regulator and policy maker.
  • Encourages private participation in non-core areas.

5. Global Enterprises (MNCs)

These companies operate in multiple countries and have huge capital and advanced technology. They produce on a large scale.

Example: Coca-Cola, Samsung, Microsoft.

Advantages:
  • Brings foreign investment and technology.
  • Creates jobs and boosts economy.
  • Promotes international business standards.
Disadvantages:
  • Can exploit natural resources and labour.
  • Profits go outside the country.
  • Can dominate local industries.

6. Joint Ventures

When two or more businesses come together to start a new enterprise by sharing investment, risk, and control.

Example: Maruti Suzuki, Hero Honda (past).

Advantages:
  • Combines strengths of both partners.
  • Reduces risk and increases innovation.
  • Access to local market + global expertise.
Disadvantages:
  • Management conflicts may arise.
  • Profit sharing and control issues.

7. Public Private Partnership (PPP)

Collaboration between government and private companies to finance and operate infrastructure projects.

Example: Metro projects, highways, hospitals.

Advantages:
  • Efficient use of resources.
  • Reduces government burden.
  • Better service delivery through private sector efficiency.
Disadvantages:
